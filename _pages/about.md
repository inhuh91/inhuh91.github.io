Hi! My name is In Huh, and I am a second-year Ph.D. student in Electrical and Computer Engineering at Purdue University, working under the supervision of Prof. <a href="https://sites.google.com/view/alam-research-group/home">Muhammad Ashraful Alam</a>. 

Modern machine learning (ML) can reproduce what physical systems look like, but it often fails to identify what they are. My research asks **how ML can learn the intrinsic structure of physical systems**, rather than merely reproduce statistical patterns of their observations. 

Instead of treating every observed difference as new information, I ask which differences are physically meaningful—such as changes in conservation laws or dynamical regimes—and which are merely changes in observational representation—such as coordinate transformations, unit rescalings, or observation distortions. This leads to **quotient representation learning**: separating intrinsic dynamical structure from representation-dependent degrees of freedom. In this view, topological invariants capture the qualitative information that must be preserved, while symmetry groups specify the transformations that should be quotiented out. 

I approach this problem from two complementary directions. **A topology-driven perspective** asks what must remain invariant for two systems to be considered physically equivalent. **A symmetry-driven perspective** asks which observed variations arise only from changes of representation and should therefore be removed. By combining these dual viewpoints, I develop ML methods that recognize when seemingly different datasets encode the same underlying physical phenomenon, with applications to nonlinear dynamical systems and semiconductor device modeling.
